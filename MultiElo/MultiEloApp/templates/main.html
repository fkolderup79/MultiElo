Why use Multiway Game Rating?

Do you have a Home Game League of any kind; poker, board game, lawn game, card game etc., and you would like to see performance of each player both long term and short term? Then the easy to use and fully customizable Multiway Game Rating can add some extra excitement to your league.

You will get a good graphic overview how each player is performing as well as other statistics.

The Multiway Home Game is taking advantage of calculations from the traditional ELO rating system to match every player in a game up against each other. That will result in a fair rating after each game regardless of how often each player is playing, the strength of the players in each game, and the number of players in each game.


Multiway Game Rating use of ELO rating

The ELO rating system is traditionally a method for calculating the relative skill levels in only two-player games. The Multiway Game Rating have improved this system to be used for games with as many players as you like. The traditional ELO calculations are still done between each and every player in a game, but additional logic is added to distribute the points based on a distribution model of choice. The ELO rating is a zero-sum calculation which means the amount of ELO points players win in a game are exactly equal to the amount of ELO points other player loose in the game. That means the average ELO rating within a league will always be exactly the ELO rating that every player started with. Whenever a player has a high rating it would require this player to perform on a high level to keep this rating.

In general terms:

  • If a high-rated player beats a low-rated player in game only a few points are given to the high-rated player.
  • If a low-rated player beats a high-rated player in a game a lot of points are given to the low-rated player.
  • If a low-rated player ties a high-rated player in a game some points are give to the low-rated player

In order to use the Multiway Game Rating there is no need to know technical details about how the calculations are done. Anyways, below are technical details for anyone interested in the actual calculations.


ELO rating traditional calculations

The ELO calculations may seem intimidating at first sight but really there are only 3 steps performed to generate traditional ELO ratings:

These are the 3 steps for traditional ELO calculations:


  1. Expected Score Calculation

    2. $$ {E_A =\frac{1}{1+10^{(R_B-R_A)/D}} } $$
    • The predicted outcome of a game based on the current ELO rating for Player A (RA) and Player B (RB) and Deviation (D).
    • The expected score value will be calculated between 0 and 1.
    • The expected score value of 0.6 for Player A means that Player A should beat Player B 60% of the time.
    • The Deviation controls the win probability based on the difference in ELO ratings (this is commonly set to 400).
    • When Deviation is set to 400, then an ELO Rating difference of 400 points means that the player with the higher ELO rating has a 10 times better chance to win than the player with the lower ELO rating.
    • When Deviation is set to 400, then an ELO Rating difference of 800 points means that the player with the higher ELO rating has a 100 times better chance to win than the player with the lower ELO rating.

  2. Actual Score

    3. $$ {S_A =X_A} $$
    • The Actual Score is represented by a value between 0 and 1.
    • Normal use of Score value is value 0 means a loss, value 1 means a win and value 0.5 means a tie.

  3. New ELO Rating Calculation

    4. $$ {R^{'}_A =R_A + K * (S_A - E_A)} $$
    • The new ELO rating will be calculated based on the current ELO rating, a K-factor, actual score and expected score
    • The K-Factor is value chosen based on how volatile the ELO rating should be. A higher K-factor means that the ELO ratings will be more volatile. Normal range for a K-Factor is between 10 and 40.
    • The Actual Score is what was set in the Actual Score section.
    • The Expected Score is what was calculated in the Expected Score calculation

Example

  • Player A: 1140 ELO rating
  • Player B: 900 ELO rating
  • K-Factor: 10
  • Deviation: 400

Expected Scores for Player A and Player B

$$ {E_A =\frac{1}{1+10^{(900-1140)/400}} = {0.80}}$$ $$ {E_B =\frac{1}{1+10^{(1140-900)/400}} = {0.20}}$$

New ELO ratings for Player A and Player B:

  • Player A wins
    • $$ {S_A =1} $$ $$ {S_B =0} $$ $$ {R^{'}_A = 1140 + 10 * (1 - {0.80}) = 1142 }$$ $$ {R^{'}_B = 900 + 10 * (0 - {0.20}) = 898 }$$

  • Player B wins

    $$ {S_A =0} $$ $$ {S_B =1} $$ $$ {R^{'}_A = 1140 + 10 * (0 - {0.80}) = 1132}$$ $$ {R^{'}_B = 900 + 10 * (1 - {0.20}) = 908}$$

  • Tie between Player A and Player B

    $$ {S_A =0.5} $$ $$ {S_B =0.5} $$ $$ {R^{'}_A = 1140 + 10 * (0.5 - {0.80}) = 1137}$$ $$ {R^{'}_B = 900 + 10 * (0.5 - {0.20}) = 903}$$

For more information about traditiontal ELO rating, I would suggest visiting this article: ELO Rating Information


Multiway Game Rating Additional calculations

The key to the Multiway Game Rating calculation is how the ELO Points are distributed. The method allows for a flexible setup how the points are distributed to best fit the game type and/or liking.

These are the options for the setup of distribution:


  1. Linear Distribution

    2. Linear Distribution gives a distribution to each position based on a linear factor. That means the distribution difference in number between each position is exactly the same. You have two further choices for Linear Distribution:

    1. All positions - will give a linear distribution to all positions except last position.
    2. Set positions - will give a linear distribution to a desired number of positions.

  2. Exponential Distribution

    3. Exponential Distribution gives a distribution to each position based on an exponential factor. That means the distribution difference in percentage between each position is exactly the same. You have two further choices for Exponential Distribution

    1. All positions - will give a exponential distribution to all position except last position.
    2. Set positions - will give a exponential distribution to a desired number of positions.

  3. Positional Distribution

    4. Positional distribution will give a set distribution based on position.

    1. Set positions - will enable to set a direct distribution per position.

Regardless of what distribution option that is chosen the model will always calculate the total distribution to be exactly 100% (which you can see in all graphs above). All these distributions options which all are totaling 100% will be used the same way to calculate the player rating.

Calculations of new ELO ratings

The base for calculating the new elo rating is for each player to gain or loose points based on the distribution of the position for that player (ties are supported). A player is gaining points from any player with a worse position and loosing points to any player with a better position. For ties the lower rated player will gain points and the higher rated player will loose points. The formulas used for the ELO points are the following:

  1. Expected Score
    2. $$ {E_A =\frac{1}{1+10^{(R_B-R_A)/400}} } $$

    Expected Score is calculated just like traditional ELO.

  2. Points gained
    3. $$ {P_A =K * (d_A - d_B) * (S_A - E_A)} $$ $$ {P_B =K * (d_B - d_A) * (S_A - E_A)} $$

    Points gained between players is calculated based on K-factor, Actual Score, Expected Score and the difference in distribution between the players.

  3. New ELO Rating
    4. $$ {R^{'}_A =R_A + \sum_{P_A}} $$

    New ELO rating for player is calculated based on original ELO rating plus points gained and lost.
